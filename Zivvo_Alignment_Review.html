How to use this: Issues are grouped by topic and rated High / Medium / Low. Questions at the end are the decisions that need to come out of your conversation. One resolved item is included for completeness — the consumer service fee was removed from the dossier prior to this review.

High severity — must resolve before any design or build work 2 issues

1 · QR codes appear throughout the mockups, contradicting the "no QR" principle HIGH
The Payments section states clearly: "No coupons, no QR codes" and "No QR; arrival matched by name." The Features section also says booking confirmation is "(no QR)". Despite this, three screens directly contradict it:
  • Screen 8 · Confirmation: Displays a large QR code with the caption "Show this QR at the desk."
  • Screen 11 · Operator Scanner: An entire operator-facing screen titled "Scan booking QR" with a live scanning UI and a "Check in & redeem" button — only possible if QR exists.
  • Journey flow Screen 4 · Confirmation: Also shows a QR code and "£54.60 Paid" label.
The Strategic Review calls this a "substantive product decision, not a cosmetic correction" — the two models (name-match vs QR) are mutually exclusive and affect both operator workflow and consumer interface. This must be resolved before any further design work.
Source conflict: index.html Payments section (text) vs Screens 8, 11, Journey Screen 4 (mockups) · Strategic Review Issue 2.2
2 · Zivvo Card is included in Phase 0 MVP — the Strategic Review says defer it HIGH
The Phase 0 MVP list in the Roadmap section explicitly includes "Zivvo Card cashback (no QR)" and the tech stack lists "Stripe Connect + Issuing" as MVP items. The RN implementation notes also include "Zivvo Card in expo-apple-wallet / Google Wallet" as part of the kit build.

The Strategic Review rates this HIGH IMPACT and recommends deferral: the Card combines card issuance, transaction-matching, cashback settlement, reconciliation, dispute liability, and Australian compliance obligations — a full fintech workstream embedded within the marketplace build. The core demand question — will consumers book discounted off-peak activities? — does not require the Card to be answered.

If the Card is deferred, the walk-in venue redemption model (currently "pay with Zivvo Card, get cashback") is also undefined. See Issue 10.
Source conflict: index.html Roadmap Phase 0 + RN notes vs Strategic Review Issue 2.1

Medium severity — misalignments that affect the dossier's credibility 8 issues

3 · All mockups, prices, and locations are UK — launch market is Melbourne, Australia MEDIUM
Every consumer-facing screen in the dossier uses British pounds (£) and London locations. Specific instances:
  • Screen 3 (Home): "📍 Shoreditch, London"
  • Screen 4 (Map): "Deals near Shoreditch" · filter chip shows "£"
  • Screen 5 (Venue): "Strike Lanes — Shoreditch" · prices in £
  • Screen 8 (Confirmation): "Strike Lanes — Shoreditch"
  • Typography example: "Strike Lanes — Shoreditch" · prices in £
  • Onboarding: "£14 avg saving"
  • Journey flow: "📍 Shoreditch" throughout
The Strategic Review confirms Melbourne as the launch market and notes that the UK-oriented styling should be retired so that venue-facing materials present an Australian product. This matters for early venue conversations — pitching with London-branded screens to Melbourne operators is a credibility issue.
Source conflict: All mockup screens (index.html) vs Strategic Review Issue 2.3 · Unit Economics (AU rates)
4 · Stripe fee rates in the payments table are US rates, not Australian MEDIUM
The Provider Comparison table in the Payments section shows: Stripe Connect: "2.9% + $0.30" — labelled explicitly as "Fee (US)" in the column header. The Unit Economics spreadsheet and Strategic Review model on the correct Australian domestic card rate: 1.7% + A$0.30. This is a material difference — on a A$48 booking, the US rate costs A$1.69 vs the AU rate of A$1.12. The dossier's payments section is currently built on the wrong jurisdiction.
Source conflict: index.html Payments table vs Unit Economics spreadsheet (AU rates) · Strategic Review Section 4
5 · The settlement flow example uses a $40 USD booking, not A$48 AUD MEDIUM
The "Underlying settlement" flow in the Payments section works through a $40 booking with an 18% commission = $7.20, a Stripe fee of ~$1.46 (calculated at the US 2.9% + $0.30 rate), netting ~$5.74 to the platform and $32.80 to the venue.

The Strategic Review and spreadsheet model a A$48 deal price at AU rates: commission A$8.64, Stripe A$1.12, net contribution A$7.52, venue receives A$39.36. Both the booking amount and the fee rates need to be updated to reflect the actual Australian model.
Source conflict: index.html Payments flow ($40 USD / US rates) vs Strategic Review Section 4 + Unit Economics (A$48 / AU rates)
6 · Stale total of £54.60 persists in multiple screens after the service fee was removed MEDIUM
The consumer service fee (£2.60) was removed from the dossier, but the old total of £54.60 still appears in four places:
  • Screen 7 (Payment) · CTA button: "Pay £54.60 · save £32" — should now be £52
  • Screen 7 (Payment) · Klarna split: "3 × £18.20" — based on £54.60, should recalculate
  • Screen 8 (Confirmation) · PAID field: Shows £54.60
  • Journey flow Screens 4 & 5: Total shows £54.60 and "Paid £54.60"
These are internal consistency errors that will be noticed immediately by anyone reading the dossier carefully.
Source: index.html Screens 7, 8, Journey Screens 4–5
7 · Market sizing data is US-sourced; no Australian or Melbourne-specific figures MEDIUM
The overview stat cards cite $11.9B US bowling (2024) → $16.8B by 2030 and a ~26% CAGR for the location-based entertainment market — both from US sources. The competitive benchmark for EatClub uses USD valuation figures ($27M Series B, ~$200M valuation). The dossier makes no reference to the size of the Australian entertainment market, Melbourne's leisure spend, or local venue density.

For a Melbourne-first launch, the relevant market data is Australian. US figures may be directionally useful but are not the operating environment.
Source: index.html Overview section · Strategic Review Issue 2.3
8 · The five-deal cap is presented as fixed design doctrine — it's an untested hypothesis MEDIUM
The dossier states: "Every venue lists at most five live deals at a time. Scarcity is the point." It is framed as an intentional, non-negotiable design principle — the "anti-Groupon" and a "members'-club feel."

The Strategic Review rates this LOW IMPACT but flags it explicitly: the proposition is plausible but unvalidated. For venues with deep off-peak troughs, a five-deal limit may leave incremental capacity unsold — the exact capacity the platform exists to fill. The recommendation is to implement it as a configurable default and treat it as a candidate for A/B testing once venues are live, rather than presenting it as fixed policy in the dossier.
Source conflict: index.html Features section vs Strategic Review Issue 2.4
9 · Theatre and live shows rated ★★★★★ and treated as co-equal — the review says they're de-scoped MEDIUM
The Overview's category table rates Live shows & theatre ★★★★★ — the highest possible rating — with the note "Empty seats expire at curtain-up — classic yield play." The footnote says "shows & theatre add a second, high-volume yield play."

The Strategic Review notes this has been quietly de-scoped: the product is structured around a rolling 7-day window and a live availability engine, which suit competitive-socialising venues far better than theatre — a production booking weeks ahead does not fit a 7-day window. If theatre is to be served later, it requires a distinct "upcoming events" model. The dossier should reflect this as a deliberate scoping decision, not leave theatre rated the same as bowling and escape rooms.
Source conflict: index.html Overview category table vs Strategic Review Issue 2.5
10 · If the Zivvo Card is deferred, walk-in venue redemption has no defined model MEDIUM
The dossier defines two redemption modes: pre-paid booking for reserved activities, and Zivvo Card cashback for walk-in venues (bars, restaurants, arcades). The entire walk-in model — including the "How the Zivvo Card works" flow (Steps 1–5) — is built around the Card.

If the Card is deferred per the Strategic Review's recommendation, walk-in venues have no redemption mechanism. The dossier does not address this gap. Either walk-in venues are also deferred from MVP, or a different mechanism (e.g. in-app QR, code at POS, or name-check) is needed — but this conflicts with the "no QR, no codes" principle. This needs an explicit decision.
Source: index.html Payments section (walk-in flow) · Strategic Review Issue 2.1
11 · No launch vertical is specified — all ★★★★★ categories are treated as equal MEDIUM
The dossier's roadmap does not name a launch vertical. The Strategic Review is explicit: bowling and escape rooms are the strongest candidates and recommends picking one to concentrate on in Melbourne. The dossier lists competitive socialising, adrenaline & action, and puzzles & immersive all at ★★★★★ with no guidance on which to pursue first.

This matters practically — supply acquisition, venue pitch materials, and the mock venue database all need to be built for a specific category. "All of the above" is not a launch strategy.
Source: index.html Overview + Roadmap vs Strategic Review Section 5.1

Low severity — internal consistency and detail errors 8 issues

12 · Onboarding screen claims "2,400+ venues" — no basis for this at launch LOW
Screen 1 (Onboarding) displays a social-proof bar with "2,400+ venues", "£14 avg saving", and "⚡ last-minute". At launch in Melbourne with a handful of seed venues, this figure is entirely fabricated. Presenting it to investors, partners, or early venues as part of the dossier undermines credibility. The figure and currency both need updating when actual launch scope is defined.
Source: index.html Screen 1 (Onboarding mockup)

Screen 7 (Payment): the primary CTA button reads "Pay £54.60 · save £32" — should now be £52. The Klarna option shows "3 × £18.20" which is £54.60 ÷ 3 — this also needs to recalculate to £52 ÷ 3 = £17.33. Both are stale after the service fee removal.
Source: index.html Screen 7 (Payment mockup)

Screen 8 (Booking Confirmation): the booking summary shows PAID: £54.60. This is the old total including the removed service fee. Should now read £52.
Source: index.html Screen 8 (Confirmation mockup)
15 · Journey flow confirmation screen also shows £54.60 total and a QR code LOW
In the User Journey section, the confirmation step (Screen 4 of the journey) shows a summary with Total: £54.60 and Paid: £54.60. It also shows a QR code on the confirmation screen — contradicting the no-QR principle (see Issue 1). Both the stale price and the QR need resolution.
Source: index.html Journey section, Screen 4
16 · SaaS tier pricing is in USD — should be AUD for the Australian market LOW
The future commission model section mentions a SaaS tier at "$0 / $49 / $99/mo" — using the $ symbol without specifying currency, but in the context of a US-rate document this reads as USD. For Melbourne, this should be specified as AUD and validated against Australian SaaS pricing norms.
Source: index.html Payments section — commission model
17 · Klarna/Afterpay threshold is quoted in USD ("above ~$150") LOW
The Payment Methods section recommends "Klarna/Afterpay above ~$150 (parties)". For Australia this should be in AUD. Additionally, Afterpay is Australian-founded and highly relevant to the local market — it may warrant being called out more prominently rather than listed as a secondary option.
Source: index.html Payments section — methods, deposits & compliance
18 · Defensibility is framed around the Zivvo Card and AI pricing — neither exists at launch LOW
The "Where Zivvo wins" section lists "Copy EatClub-Pay frictionless redemption" as the first strategic move — the defensible UX moat. The Strategic Review directly challenges this: "The Card is a feature, not a structural barrier, and AI-based pricing requires transaction data the platform will not possess at launch."

The review argues that real defensibility comes from local venue density, exclusivity terms, and becoming the consumer default for spontaneous leisure — none of which are designed in the dossier, only assumed. This framing needs updating so the dossier's competitive moat argument reflects what is actually achievable at launch.
Source conflict: index.html Market section ("five strategic moves") vs Strategic Review Section 3.3
19 · EatClub competitive data may be stale — benchmark should be verified before venue pitches LOW
The dossier benchmarks EatClub at "~3,000–5,000 venues, 2M+ downloads, AU + UK (London May 2025), ~$27M Series B at ~$200M valuation." This was compiled June 2025 — over a year ago. EatClub's UK expansion, venue count, and any funding updates should be verified before this data is used in venue conversations or investor materials. Using stale competitor data in a pitch is a credibility risk.
Source: index.html Market section · dossier compiled 25 Jun 2026 (note: data cited as of 2025)
20 · Consumer service fee (~5%) has been removed from the dossier RESOLVED
The original dossier included a "~5% consumer service fee" as a separate revenue line. The Strategic Review and Unit Economics model both treat revenue as venue commission only — the consumer pays a single clean price with all costs built in. The fee box has been removed from the commission model section, and checkout totals in the booking builder have been updated to reflect the no-fee model. Remaining stale prices (Issues 13–15) are a follow-on from this change.
Source: resolved prior to this review

Questions to answer in your discussion 16 questions

These are the decisions that need to come out of the Pulkit conversation. Each unresolved question blocks something downstream.

Product & design decisions

1
QR or no-QR — which is the actual redemption model? The text says name-match, the mockups show QR scanning. These cannot coexist. If name-match: Screens 8 and 11 need to be redrawn. If QR: the text needs to change and the "frictionless, no-staff-training" advantage disappears.
Product
2
Is the Zivvo Card in or out of MVP? The Strategic Review recommends deferring it to reduce build complexity and validate demand first. If it's deferred, what replaces it for walk-in venues? If it stays, who is building the fintech compliance side?
Product
3
If the Card is deferred, what is the walk-in venue redemption model? Currently the entire walk-in flow depends on the Card. Options are: (a) defer walk-in venues entirely from MVP; (b) use a code or QR — but this contradicts the no-QR position; (c) some other mechanism. Needs an explicit answer.
Product
4
Is the five-deal cap per venue fixed policy or a configurable default to A/B test? The dossier treats it as gospel. The Review says it's a hypothesis that might leave capacity unsold. Should it be presented as a design principle or a starting point?
Product
5
Is theatre explicitly de-scoped from the MVP and Phase 1? It's rated ★★★★★ in the overview but doesn't fit the 7-day rolling window. If it's deferred, should it be marked as a separate future model in the dossier rather than listed alongside bowling and escape rooms?
Product
6
What is the exact MVP feature list — and what is explicitly excluded? The Strategic Review recommends a frozen one-page first-release feature list with an explicit deferral list. Points, referrals, and reviews are currently in Phase 0 — are these actually MVP or nice-to-have?

Numbers & economics

13
What is the realistic average order value for the launch vertical in Melbourne? The Unit Economics model is highly sensitive to average order value. The standard example uses A$48, the group example A$120. What do bowling lanes and escape rooms actually charge in Melbourne, and what is the expected party size?

14
Has GST been factored into the Australian unit economics? The Strategic Review flags that "Australian payment-processing fees, local interchange rates and GST treatment are not reflected in the figures presented." The Unit Economics spreadsheet uses pre-tax figures. How does GST affect the commission model and what the venue actually receives?

The Strategic Review says break-even requires "a few hundred bookings per month." Has this been modelled against real cost estimates (AWS, Algolia, Stripe Connect monthly fees, etc.)?
